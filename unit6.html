Q2. Short Answer Questions

i. What is Radio Transceiver?
A radio transceiver is a wireless communication device that combines both a transmitter and a receiver in a single unit. It is designed to send as well as receive data through radio signals. Transceivers are used in mobile phones, two-way radios, and wireless networking equipment. They operate in full-duplex mode, allowing simultaneous sending and receiving of data, making them essential for modern telecommunications.

ii. What is 'Hotspot'?
A hotspot is a physical location where people can access the Internet using Wi-Fi technology via a Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN). It is typically created by a wireless router or an access point connected to an Internet Service Provider (ISP). Hotspots are commonly found in public places like cafes, airports, and hotels. They allow devices like smartphones and laptops to connect wirelessly within a range of about 20 meters indoors.

iii. What does IEEE stand for?
IEEE stands for the "Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers." It is a professional organization and a leading developer of international standards that underpin many of today's telecommunications, information technology, and power generation products. In the context of wireless networks, the IEEE 802.11 standard is the most famous as it defines the protocols for Wi-Fi communication used worldwide.

iv. What is meant by line-of-sight communication?
Line-of-sight (LoS) communication is a type of propagation that requires the transmitter and receiver to be in a direct, unobstructed path of each other. This means the radio waves travel in a straight line without being blocked by buildings, hills, or the curvature of the earth. It is commonly used in high-frequency radio waves, microwave transmission, and infrared technology, where any physical obstacle can interrupt the signal.

v. Differentiate between short distance and long distance wireless communication.
Short-distance wireless communication refers to technologies designed for localized areas, typically within a few meters or a building (e.g., Bluetooth, Infrared, and Wi-Fi). It is used for connecting personal devices. In contrast, long-distance wireless communication covers thousands of kilometers and uses satellites, cellular towers, or high-power microwave links (e.g., GPS, GSM, and Satellite TV). Long-distance systems require complex infrastructure like base stations and orbital satellites.

vi. What is Global Positioning System (GPS)?
GPS is a satellite-based navigation system that provides precise location and time information anywhere on Earth. It consists of a constellation of 24 satellites orbiting the planet. A GPS receiver calculates its position by timing the signals sent by these satellites. It is widely used in aviation, marine navigation, car tracking, and smartphone maps, and it operates 24 hours a day in all weather conditions without any subscription fee.

vii. What is Wireless Markup Language?
Wireless Markup Language (WML) is a specialized markup language designed for devices that implement the Wireless Application Protocol (WAP), such as older mobile phones and pagers. Since these devices have small screens and limited bandwidth, WML optimizes the display of text and images. It is based on XML and was the first markup standard for the mobile internet before modern smartphones started supporting full HTML.

viii. What is Wireless Application Protocol?
Wireless Application Protocol (WAP) is an open standard protocol that enables wireless devices like mobile phones and PDAs to access the Internet. It acts as a bridge between the wireless network and the standard Internet protocols. WAP was developed to deliver Internet content to mobile devices with limited processing power and small displays, ensuring that users can access web pages and emails while on the move.

ix. Write short note on Web Protocol Stack?
The Web Protocol Stack is a set of layered protocols used to define, locate, and implement web services. In the wireless context, it includes the Transport Protocol (handling data transfer), Messaging Protocol (encoding messages in XML/SOAP), Description Protocol (using WSDL to describe services), and Discovery Protocol (using UDDI to find services). Together, these layers ensure that different devices can communicate and exchange data over the web efficiently.

Q3. Detailed Answer Questions

i. What are the advantages and disadvantages of wireless networks?
Advantages:
1. Manageability: Users can connect easily without physical cables, making the network easier to manage and expand.
2. Mobility: Users are not tied to a specific location; they can access the network from anywhere within the signal range.
3. Fast Setup: Wireless networks are quicker to install as there is no need for extensive cabling through walls or floors.
4. Cost-Effective: Eliminates the recurring costs of wiring and maintenance of physical cables in large buildings.
5. Expandability: Adding new users is simple; as long as they have a wireless adapter, they can join the network immediately.
Disadvantages:
1. Security: Wireless signals travel through the air, making them more vulnerable to hacking and unauthorized access.
2. Interference: Signals can be blocked by walls, furniture, or interference from other electronic devices like microwaves.
3. Speed: Wireless networks are generally slower than wired (Ethernet) connections, especially when multiple users are connected.
4. Range: The coverage area is limited. Users far from the access point may experience weak signals or frequent disconnections.

ii. Explain different types of short distance communication technologies.
Short-distance communication technologies are designed for localized data transfer. Key types include:
1. Wi-Fi: Based on the IEEE 802.11 standard, it provides high-speed internet and network access within a range of 20-100 meters. It is the most common technology for homes and offices.
2. Bluetooth: A low-power microwave technology used for connecting devices like phones, laptops, and headsets over very short distances (up to 10 meters). It is ideal for personal area networks (PAN).
3. Infrared (IR): Uses light waves for communication. It requires a direct line-of-sight and is used in TV remote controls and some secure office environments. It cannot penetrate walls.
4. WiMax (Short Range): While often long-range, it can be used to provide high-speed connectivity to campuses, offering better performance than standard Wi-Fi in specific settings.

iii. Describe long distance communication technologies in detail.
Long-distance technologies cover vast geographical areas. Major types are:
1. Cellular Communication: Uses a network of "cells" or base stations (towers) to provide voice and data services to millions of mobile users. Each cell covers a specific area and connects to a central exchange.
2. Satellite Communication: Involves sending signals from Earth to satellites in orbit, which then beam them back to other locations. It is used for GPS, global TV broadcasting, and internet in remote areas.
3. Microwave Links: Uses high-frequency radio beams for point-to-point communication between towers. It requires line-of-sight and is often used by telecommunication companies to bridge long distances where cables cannot be laid.
4. Global Positioning System (GPS): A specialized long-distance system for navigation and timing using a 24-satellite constellation.

iv. Explain different classifications of Satellite Systems.
Satellites are classified based on their orbital altitude:
1. Geostationary Earth Orbit (GEO): These satellites are positioned at 22,200 miles (35,786 km) above the equator. They appear stationary from Earth because their orbital speed matches Earth's rotation. Used for TV and weather monitoring.
2. Medium Earth Orbit (MEO): Located between 1,000 and 22,200 miles. MEO satellites are mainly used for navigation systems like GPS. They cover a larger area than LEO but have some signal delay compared to them.
3. Low Earth Orbit (LEO): These are close to Earth (500 to 2,000 km). They are used for high-speed satellite internet and remote sensing. Because they are close, they have very low latency but require many satellites to provide global coverage.

v. What are the Requirements of Mobile Communication System?
A mobile communication system requires several components to function effectively:
1. Cell Sites: Physical locations with antennas and radio equipment that provide coverage within a specific "cell."
2. Mobile Subscriber Units (MSUs): The actual devices used by people, such as smartphones or tablets, equipped with transceivers.
3. Mobile Station (MS): Includes the mobile equipment and the Subscriber Identity Module (SIM) card that stores user information.
4. Network Infrastructure: Includes Mobile Switching Centers (MSC) that handle call routing, handovers between cells, and connection to the public telephone network (PSTN).
5. Radio Frequency Spectrum: Licensed bands of frequencies allowed for transmitting signals without interference.

vi. Give important features and limitations of Mobile Communication System.
Features:
1. User Mobility: Allows users to communicate while moving between different geographical locations.
2. Device Portability: Modern mobile devices are small, lightweight, and battery-powered for easy carrying.
3. Roaming: Enables users to maintain service even when traveling outside their home network's coverage area.
4. Multimedia Support: Modern 4G/5G systems support high-speed data, video calling, and streaming.
Limitations:
1. Power Issue: Mobile devices rely on batteries, which need frequent recharging and limit usage time.
2. Interference: Weather, terrain (mountains), and tall buildings can cause signal drops or poor call quality.
3. Health Hazards: There are ongoing debates and concerns about the long-term effects of radio frequency radiation on human health.
4. Bandwidth Limits: Wireless networks have limited capacity compared to fiber-optic cables, leading to congestion in crowded areas.
