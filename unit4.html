Q2. Give short answers to the following questions.

i. Differentiate between CPU and System unit.
The System Unit is the main case or "box" of a computer that houses all the internal electronic components like the motherboard, power supply, and hard drive. In contrast, the CPU (Central Processing Unit) is a small microprocessor chip located inside the system unit on the motherboard. While the system unit provides the protective structure and housing for everything, the CPU is specifically responsible for executing instructions and processing data. Essentially, the CPU is a part of the system unit, but the system unit is the entire container.

ii. Define two main types of computer casings.
Computer casings, also known as chassis, come in two primary types: Tower and Desktop. Tower Casings are vertical units designed to stand upright on the floor or a desk, providing more space for expansion and better airflow. Desktop Casings are horizontal units designed to lie flat on a surface, often with the monitor placed on top to save space. While tower cases are more popular today for their upgradeability, desktop cases were traditionally used in offices where desk space was limited.

iii. List different parts of a motherboard.
The motherboard is the main printed circuit board of a computer. Its key parts include: 1. CPU Socket (where the processor is installed), 2. RAM Slots (for memory modules), 3. Chipset (Northbridge and Southbridge), 4. Expansion Slots (PCI, PCIe, AGP), 5. BIOS/UEFI chip, 6. SATA/IDE connectors for storage, 7. Power Connectors, and 8. CMOS Battery. It also contains various ports like USB, HDMI, and Audio jacks on the back panel. All these components communicate with each other through the motherboard's circuitry.

iv. What is the role of BIOS in computer system?
BIOS (Basic Input/Output System) is a firmware built into a small chip on the motherboard. Its primary role is to perform the POST (Power-On Self-Test) when the computer starts, ensuring all hardware like RAM and keyboard are working. After the test, BIOS locates the boot loader on the hard drive and hands over control to the Operating System. It also provides a setup utility where users can configure hardware settings like boot priority and system clock. Without BIOS, the computer hardware would not know how to wake up and start the software.

v. Differentiate between a port and a slot.
A Port is an external socket located on the outside of the system unit (usually at the back or front) used to connect peripheral devices like printers, mice, and keyboards via cables. A Slot (or Expansion Slot) is an internal socket located directly on the motherboard inside the system unit. Slots are used to plug in circuit boards or expansion cards, such as graphics cards or sound cards, to add new capabilities to the computer. Briefly, ports are for external cable connections, while slots are for internal hardware upgrades.

vi. Name different types of data cables with their purpose of use.
Various data cables are used inside and outside the system unit: 1. SATA Cables are used to connect modern hard drives and SSDs to the motherboard. 2. IDE/PATA Cables were used for older storage devices. 3. USB Cables connect external devices like phones and cameras. 4. HDMI/VGA Cables are used to transmit video signals to monitors. 5. Ethernet Cables are for network connections. Each cable is designed with a specific connector shape and speed capacity to handle the transfer of data between different hardware parts.

vii. Differentiate between SIMM and DIMM.
SIMM stands for Single In-line Memory Module, which has pins on only one side of the circuit board, meaning identical signals are carried on both sides. DIMM stands for Dual In-line Memory Module, which has independent electrical contacts on each side of the board. DIMMs are modern memory modules that support 64-bit data paths, making them much faster than the older 32-bit SIMMs. Most modern computers use DIMMs (like DDR4 or DDR5) because they provide higher capacity and better performance for multitasking.

viii. Why cooling system is important for a computer?
A cooling system is vital because electronic components, especially the CPU and GPU, generate a significant amount of heat during operation. If this heat is not removed, it can lead to "thermal throttling," where the computer slows down to protect itself, or even permanent hardware damage. Cooling systems, which include heat sinks, fans, and sometimes liquid cooling, maintain a safe operating temperature. Good airflow ensures the longevity of the components and prevents the system from crashing or restarting unexpectedly due to overheating.

Q3. Give detailed answers to the following questions.

i. Discuss different types of expansion cards.

Expansion cards are circuit boards that are plugged into expansion slots on the motherboard to improve or add functionalities to a computer.

  • Video Card (Graphics Card): Processes and outputs images to the monitor. High-end cards are essential for 3D gaming and video editing.
  • Sound Card: Handles all audio input (from microphones) and output (to speakers/headphones). Many modern motherboards have built-in sound, but dedicated cards provide better quality.
  • Network Interface Card (NIC): Allows the computer to connect to a network or the internet via an Ethernet cable or Wi-Fi.
  • Modem Card: An older type of card used to connect to the internet over telephone lines.

ii. What is port? Explain different types of ports in computers.

A port is an interface on the system unit that allows external devices to be connected to the computer via cables.

Common Types of Ports:

  • USB (Universal Serial Bus): The most versatile port used for mice, keyboards, printers, and flash drives.
  • HDMI (High-Definition Multimedia Interface): Transmits high-quality digital video and audio to monitors and TVs.
  • VGA/DVI: Older analog and digital ports for connecting monitors.
  • Ethernet (RJ-45): Used for wired internet and local area network connections.
  • Audio Jacks: Small circular ports for connecting speakers, headphones, and microphones.

iii. Explain different types of ribbon cables.

Ribbon cables are flat, thin cables used inside the system unit to connect internal peripherals to the motherboard.

1. IDE/PATA Cables

Older 40-pin or 80-pin cables used to connect hard drives and CD/DVD drives. They are wide and can often block airflow inside the case.

2. Floppy Drive Cables

Similar to IDE but smaller (34 pins), used specifically for connecting floppy disk drives in older systems.

3. SATA Cables

The modern standard for storage. Although not strictly "ribbons" (they are much thinner), they replaced wide ribbon cables because they are easier to manage and allow better cooling.

iv. Discuss different types of memory chips.

Memory chips are integrated circuits that store data. There are several types used in a computer:

1. RAM Chips

Volatile memory used to store data for active processing. It comes in modules like SIMM and DIMM.

2. ROM Chips

Non-volatile memory that stores permanent instructions. Types include PROM, EPROM, and EEPROM.

3. CMOS Chip

A small memory chip that stores system configuration data (like date and time). It is powered by a small battery so it doesn't lose data when the computer is off.

4. Cache Memory

High-speed chips located very close to the CPU to speed up data access.

v. What is Bus? Explain different types of buses in computers.

A Bus is a set of parallel electrical conducting paths or wires on the motherboard that allow different components of a computer, such as the CPU, Memory, and I/O devices, to communicate with each other. The speed of a bus is measured in Megahertz (MHz), and its width (e.g., 32-bit or 64-bit) determines how much data can be sent at once. Without buses, the internal parts of a computer would remain isolated and unable to exchange information.

1. System Bus (Internal Bus)

The System Bus connects the CPU to the main memory (RAM). It is the most critical pathway in the computer and is divided into three functional parts:

  • Data Bus: A bi-directional path used to carry actual data bits between the CPU and memory. A wider data bus means faster data transfer.
  • Address Bus: A uni-directional path used by the CPU to specify the memory location it wants to read from or write to.
  • Control Bus: Carries command signals (like Read/Write) and timing signals to synchronize all hardware activities.

2. Expansion Bus (External Bus)

The Expansion Bus allows the CPU to communicate with peripheral devices and expansion cards plugged into the motherboard. Common types include:

  • PCI (Peripheral Component Interconnect): A standard bus for older sound and network cards.
  • PCIe (PCI Express): The modern, high-speed serial bus used for graphics cards and NVMe SSDs.
  • USB Bus: Used for connecting external devices like keyboards and printers.

vi. Write note on SDRAM and SRAM. Also write the detail of DDR types.

Memory chips are categorized based on their speed and how they handle electrical charges. SDRAM (Synchronous DRAM) is a type of RAM that is synchronized with the system clock, allowing it to handle instructions much faster than older DRAM. SRAM (Static RAM) is even faster because it does not need to be refreshed constantly to hold data; it is typically used for CPU Cache. SRAM is expensive but offers the highest performance among all RAM types.

Details of DDR (Double Data Rate) Types:

DDR technology is an advancement of SDRAM that allows the memory to transfer data on both the rising and falling edges of the clock signal, effectively doubling the speed. Over the years, several versions have been developed:

  • DDR1: The original version that replaced standard SDRAM. It provided higher bandwidth but consumed more power compared to modern standards.
  • DDR2: Improved upon DDR1 by doubling the data transfer rate again and reducing power consumption. It used 240-pin DIMM slots.
  • DDR3: Offered even higher speeds and lower voltage (1.5V), making it popular for many years in laptops and desktop computers.
  • DDR4: The current widely used standard. It offers significantly higher frequencies (up to 3200MHz+) and operates at a lower 1.2V, providing better efficiency and higher capacities.
  • DDR5: The latest generation designed for modern high-end processors. It offers massive bandwidth and built-in error correction (ECC), making it ideal for gaming and professional workstations.
